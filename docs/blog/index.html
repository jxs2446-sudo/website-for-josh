Blog Post 1 | What does a scientist actually do? | 18-03-2026

In Jeff Leek’s How To Be A Modern Scientist, some of the mysticism revolving around the responsibilities of the contemporary scientific thinker was dispelled for me, in addition to some of the anxiety regarding how to begin the journey to becoming one. For a long time I thought of science as only taking place in obscure academic corners accessible only to the top thinkers in each field (as measured by the number of publications they can point to). I also had little clue as to what differentiated a ‘real’ scientist from someone who is merely competent enough to use the internet or some other seemingly credible source when communicating new or innovative ideas. When we see ‘representatives’ of science in fictional media, none of the daily effort and practice required to become someone capable of contributing original knowledge is ever shown. Jeff Leek’s work revealed to me that good scientific inquiry is carried out in many deceptively simple but powerful forms: blog posts, social media, and reproducible and open-source code are all increasingly necessary avenues for scientific thinkers to grow an audience and establish their work. Carrying out these kinds of duties is what separates the modern scientist from the more isolated view of the scientist I held. In terms of small actionable steps for actually growing into the role of the modern scientist, two points that stuck out to me as being immediately actionable were creating publication-ready plots as the first-step for any future writing, and to take advantage of pre-print websites to avoid the frustrating timelines involved when attempting to write a paper. Both of these are going to be steps I take for my work in the Wilson lab at Case, for when we have optimized an assay and solution for the neonatal small analyte testing we are working on.

Blog Post 2 | Confessions from Animal Tech 27 | 20-03-2026

Between April 2024 and March 2026, I worked as an Animal Husbandry Technician at Case Western Reserve University’s Animal Resource Center. I worked in the HSAF facility in the basement of the Biomedical Research Building and was responsible for between two and four mouse rooms, each housing somewhere between 300 and 700 cages of research mice. I can say with confidence that this two-year period was one of the most miserable of my entire life. There are myriad reasons for this and I will not pretend to be faultless when it comes to some of the pain I experienced, but much of it came directly from the work I was doing. I should establish that I’ve had plenty of shit work in my life. I worked for four years as a server at a Japanese steakhouse (Hibachi Express in Columbus, Ohio) to pay my way through undergrad and felt awful through almost every shift. There was the constant stress of being harried by impatient customers and emotionally abused by an incompetent manager. I was perpetually covered in grease, oil, and the smell of industrial cleaner combined with old fryer fumes. I was always exhausted and felt supremely ugly at work. I was threatened multiple times and was either cursed at or belittled daily. At the very least, though, there were free meals and I made enough to get a car and pay my tuition.

The caliber of suffering I would learn at CWRU’s ARC was unlike anything I had experienced and unlike some of the bottom-of-the-totem-pole work I’d done in the past like when I was a server, the benefits never felt worthwhile enough to continue enduring the work—even after two years of giving the place all the energy and effort I could manage. When I interviewed for the position with manager “V” in March of 2024, I was a naive 23 year old desperate to get involved in research. I had been brutally rejected by multiple labs and felt that I was too unqualified to exercise or share my intellect. I hadn’t prepared adequately or exposed myself to research in the slightest while at college, so I had no experience on the bench or at a computer doing analysis, but I still believed I had some value to share and that it would be a waste not to take advantage of my Boston College Biology degree. In retrospect, I was both wrong and right. The right opportunity hadn’t knocked on my door yet, and I wasn’t ready for the opportunities that had come my way. In early 2024 I had reached the end of my rope after four months of unemployment, and even though I was taking a 50% pay cut (32/hr as a Clinical Data Specialist doing essentially nothing at IQVIA to 16.50 as an Animal Husbandry Technician at the ARC busting my ass) I figured the experience would more than make up for it.

The principal investigators, research fellows, and assistants who work in the BRB likely have no clue what kind of treatment the Animal Technicians responsible for overseeing their precious research animals experience on a daily basis. I will outline that experience as thoroughly as I can, with the hope that someday, maybe someone with the power to change the ARC will do so. It’s too much to encapsulate in a single blog post, but I’ll work on capturing the essence of the place over time. I’m still in contact with some of its current and past employees, and collecting their testimonies is vital to ensure an accurate depiction. Additionally, it will take time for me to accumulate satisfactory evidence to prove what I say is true. For now, we can only heed my word as a former employee.

Work begins every day at 6:30 am, though most clock in at 6:35 am what with there being a 5-minute leniency window at each clock-in or clock-out point. The less time spent in the facility, the better. This unspoken rule is the prevailing attitude of the ARC regardless if one is an animal technician, cagewash technician, a part of veterinary services, or one of the researchers. This sentiment is pervasive in the culture of the facility despite the animal technician schedule being utterly void of work-life-balance; for a place that is so obviously insufferable to each and every person there including upper management, upper management seemingly has no issue with trapping its employees at the facility for as long as possible and without any real justification. Once a month animal technicians are required to work 14 days in a row. Christmas and Thanksgiving force an unlucky team of technicians to appear for work at the facility but not a single management personnel is required to work a weekend or a holiday shift. Clock-out on a regular day is 3:00 pm, so 2:55 pm for the majority of the technicians. There is a one-hour lunch break between 10:55 am and 11:05 pm (when taking advantage of the 5-minute leniency window). We are essential employees according to the university and must be present for work through snow storms, power outages, active threats on campus, etc. Failure to appear regardless of reason (it doesn’t matter if you’re sick) results in a penalty in the form of points. Parking must be paid for, and those who do not clock in on-time or skip their weekend or holiday shifts are penalized severely through the point system.

In a way, the very first thing the ARC teaches its animal technicians is that their time is no longer theirs, and that they are being strictly monitored for complete adherence to an archaic, arbitrary schedule that serves to benefit people who not only do not care about the animal technicians facilitating their research work, but that also fail repeatedly to care about the animals they use in their research (more on that to come.) The points system is the clearest evidence of this simple but damning truth.

Every time an employee calls out of the facility, they receive 3 points. At 18 points, an employee receives a positive corrective action, barring them from promotion for at least a year. Additionally, if they receive any more points during this probationary period, they stand to be suspended without pay for a week, and if any more points accumulate after that, the individual is fired. A single late punch-in or punch-out in the morning, before or after lunch, or at the end of the day also results in a point. Calling off after a weekend or before or after a holiday is double points, and receiving points in the same way (i.e., calling off on a Tuesday twice in the same month) also results in double points.
